\subsection*{Question 1 \quad Research Question}
%----------------------------------------------------------------------------------------

\textbf{(a) Research question:}
Does participating in a company-sponsored job training program causally increase workers'
monthly wages?
This question matters because job training is a common labour market policy aimed at
raising worker productivity and reducing wage inequality.
Understanding whether training \emph{causes} higher wages --- rather than merely selecting
workers who would have earned more regardless --- is essential for evaluating its social
return.

\textbf{(b) Treatment variable:}
$D_i = 1$ if worker $i$ participated in job training in the past year; $= 0$ otherwise.
(\texttt{training} in the dataset)

\textbf{(c) Outcome variable:}
$Y_i = \log(\text{monthly wage}_i)$ in NTD. (\texttt{lnwage} in the dataset)
Using the log transformation allows the treatment coefficient to be interpreted as an
approximate percentage change in wages.

\bigskip\horrule{0.4pt}\bigskip

The dataset is a cross-sectional survey of \textbf{500 workers} employed across

\subsection*{Question 7 \quad Simple Regression}
%----------------------------------------------------------------------------------------

We estimate the following \textbf{short regression} (no controls):
\begin{equation}
  Y_i = \delta + \alpha \, D_i + u_i
  \label{eq:short}
\end{equation}
where $Y_i = \log(\text{wage}_i)$, $D_i$ is the training dummy,
$\alpha$ is the average log-wage difference between trained and untrained workers,
and $u_i$ captures all other determinants of wages.

As shown in Column~(1) of Table~\ref{tab:reg}, $\hat{\alpha} = 0.291^{***}$ (s.e.\ 0.040),
implying that trained workers earn approximately \textbf{29.1\% more} than untrained workers.

\bigskip\horrule{0.4pt}\bigskip

%----------------------------------------------------------------------------------------
\subsection*{Question 8 \quad Causal Interpretation and OVB}
%----------------------------------------------------------------------------------------

The estimate from Question~7 is \textbf{unlikely to be causal}.
Workers who participate in training are not randomly assigned: workers with more education
are simultaneously more likely to receive training and to command higher wages,
creating a spurious correlation between $D_i$ and $Y_i$.

\textbf{Omitted variable: Education} ($X_i = \texttt{edu}_i$)
\begin{itemize}
  \item \textit{Relevance}: Education raises wages
        ($\text{Cov}(X_i,\, u_i) \neq 0$ because $\beta_{\text{edu}} > 0$).
  \item \textit{Endogeneity}: More-educated workers are more likely to receive training
        ($\text{Cov}(X_i,\, D_i) > 0$, i.e.\ $\pi > 0$ in the auxiliary regression below).
\end{itemize}

Because both conditions hold and in the same direction,
omitting education causes $\hat{\alpha}$ in Equation~\eqref{eq:short} to be
\textbf{upward biased}: it partly captures the education wage premium and attributes it
incorrectly to training.

\bigskip\horrule{0.4pt}\bigskip

%----------------------------------------------------------------------------------------
\subsection*{Question 9 \quad OVB Formula}
%----------------------------------------------------------------------------------------

The \textbf{long regression} adds education as a control:
\begin{equation}
  Y_i = \delta + \alpha \, D_i + \beta \, X_i + \varepsilon_i
  \label{eq:long}
\end{equation}
The OVB formula shows that the short-regression estimator converges to:
\begin{equation}
  \hat{\alpha}_{\text{short}} \;\overset{p}{\longrightarrow}\;
    \alpha + \underbrace{\beta}_{\substack{\text{effect of edu}\\\text{on }Y_i}}
            \times
            \underbrace{\dfrac{\text{Cov}(X_i, D_i)}{V(D_i)}}_{\substack{= \,\pi:\;\text{coef.\ of }D_i\\\text{in }X_i = \gamma_0 + \pi D_i + \eta_i}}
  \label{eq:ovb}
\end{equation}
where $\beta > 0$ (education raises wages) and $\pi > 0$ (trained workers are more educated),
so OVB $= \beta\,\pi > 0$: the simple regression \textbf{overestimates} the training effect.

\medskip
\textbf{Numerical verification:}
From the data,
$\hat{\beta} = 0.073$ (slope of $Y_i$ on $X_i$ in the long regression)
and $\hat{\pi} = 1.177$ (slope of $X_i$ on $D_i$ in the auxiliary regression),
giving $\widehat{\text{OVB}} = 0.073 \times 1.177 \approx \mathbf{0.086}$.
This matches the observed drop: $0.291 - 0.205 = 0.086$. \checkmark

\bigskip\horrule{0.4pt}\bigskip

%----------------------------------------------------------------------------------------
\subsection*{Question 10 \quad Adding Control Variables}
%----------------------------------------------------------------------------------------

We progressively add control variables $X_i = (\texttt{edu}_i,\,\texttt{age}_i,\,\texttt{female}_i)$
to the regression:
\begin{equation}
  Y_i = \delta + \alpha \, D_i + \beta_1\,\texttt{edu}_i
        + \beta_2\,\texttt{age}_i + \beta_3\,\texttt{female}_i + \varepsilon_i
  \label{eq:controls}
\end{equation}

Results are shown in Columns~(1)--(4) of Table~\ref{tab:reg}.
Adding education alone (Column~2) causes the training coefficient to fall sharply from
0.291 to \textbf{0.205}, consistent with the upward OVB in Questions~8 and~9.
Adding age further (Column~3) slightly reverses the drop (to 0.219): this is because
age is \emph{negatively} correlated with training (older workers are less likely to
receive training) but \emph{positively} affects wages, so omitting age created a
small downward bias.
Adding gender (Column~4) brings the estimate down to \textbf{0.189}.

\bigskip\horrule{0.4pt}\bigskip

%----------------------------------------------------------------------------------------
\subsection*{Question 11 \quad Bad Controls}
%----------------------------------------------------------------------------------------

The three control variables are \textbf{not bad controls} because they are all
\emph{predetermined} --- determined before the training decision and not themselves
affected by training.

\begin{itemize}
  \item \texttt{edu}: Schooling is largely completed before workers enter a firm;
        the training here is in-firm occupational training, not a schooling programme.
  \item \texttt{age}: Fixed at birth; training cannot change a worker's age.
  \item \texttt{female}: Biological sex is unaffected by training status.
\end{itemize}

Including these variables removes wage variation driven by pre-existing individual
characteristics, yielding a cleaner estimate of the causal training effect.

\bigskip\horrule{0.4pt}\bigskip

%----------------------------------------------------------------------------------------
\subsection*{Question 12 \quad Fixed Effects}
%----------------------------------------------------------------------------------------

We add \textbf{firm fixed effects} $\lambda_j$ to absorb all time-invariant,
firm-level unobservables:
\begin{equation}
  Y_{ij} = \lambda_j + \alpha \, D_{ij}
         + \beta_1\,\texttt{edu}_{ij} + \beta_2\,\texttt{age}_{ij}
         + \beta_3\,\texttt{female}_{ij} + \varepsilon_{ij}
  \label{eq:fe}
\end{equation}
where $i$ indexes workers and $j$ indexes firms.
Firm fixed effects control for unobserved factors such as management quality,
industry wage premia, and firm-level training culture that simultaneously affect
wages and the likelihood of offering training.

As shown in Column~(5) of Table~\ref{tab:reg}, adding firm fixed effects reduces
$\hat{\alpha}$ from 0.189 to \textbf{0.159} (standard errors clustered by firm),
suggesting that part of the residual estimate in Column~(4) still reflected
sorting: higher-quality firms pay more \emph{and} are more likely to offer training.
